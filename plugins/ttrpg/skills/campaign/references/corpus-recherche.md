## Mapping the relationships between characters (relationship map / R-map, Ron Edwards)

**Definition.** A diagram that places the characters (players and NPCs) as nodes and connects those nodes with lines representing their ties. Formalized by Ron Edwards in The Sorcerer's Soul (a supplement for Sorcerer), the R-map originally described only blood/family and sexual ties; later formulations broadened it to any tie important to the situation (debt, loyalty, rivalry, possession of an object, place). The map establishes the connections but dictates no action by itself: it is a support for a pre-existing situation, not a scripted plot.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM proceeds as follows: 1) List the player characters and all the NPCs who matter for the initial situation, as nodes. 2) Draw the ties: who loves/hates/owes/controls/desires whom. Relationships are often asymmetrical (A wants to eliminate B, B is indifferent) — indicate this with directed arrows and a color code or symbols (neutral/positive/negative/lethal). 3) Ensure the map is dense and loaded: each NPC must have at least one tense tie that pre-exists the PCs' arrival, so that the situation is unstable and ready to explode. 4) Anchor the PCs INSIDE the map (a PC must touch several nodes) rather than leaving them external. 5) Derive the bangs from the map: every unresolved tension in the R-map becomes a seed for a "bang" (a moment forcing a PC to choose/act) that the GM stores in their "bandolier of bangs." The map is therefore the upstream tool that makes the "situation" concrete and mechanically generates the raw material of play, without pre-scripting what the players will do.

**Variants.**
- Original R-map (Sorcerer's Soul): strictly family and sexual ties, to produce a charged dramatic situation
- Expanded R-map: any relevant tie (debt, loyalty, hatred, control, secret), characters + places + key objects on the same map
- Edge coding by color/symbol for the type and intensity of the tie (neutral, positive, negative, lethal intent)
- Asymmetrical directed edges (the A→B tie differs from the B→A tie)
- Tracking favors/debts: numbered arrows (0 = nothing owed, 1 = small favor, 2 = major favor, 3 = life debt), with the debts of a dead character transferring to their killer
- Map of factions/groups rather than individuals (useful for politics, e.g. Vampire)
- Living map: ties are annotated/rewritten as play makes the relationships evolve

**System-agnostic:** yes — A preparation technique independent of the rules: it structures the fiction (who is tied to whom) upstream and depends on no sub-mechanic. Transferable to any system, including a simulationist game like Zombiology (mapping survivors, factions, coveted resources, and pre-outbreak grudges produces exploitable tensions). Born in the Forge/narrativist movement around Sorcerer, but with no PbtA or story-game exclusivity.

**Forge equivalent.** A central building block of "Situation" in the Forge vocabulary (Edwards): the R-map materializes the charged, unstable Situation, from which one then draws the "Bangs" (decisive moments forcing a choice on the PC). It articulates with the "Kicker" (the initial event that launches the character) and the "bandolier of bangs." It is also kin to Color/Backstory: what is true before the players act.

## Imagining a mirror NPC (a character who reflects or inverts the PC)

**Definition.** Designing an NPC deliberately built in a relationship of contrast or resemblance with a PC, so as to illuminate the PC's traits, values, or choices. The resemblance-mirror shares the PC's dilemma but has resolved it differently (what the PC could become); the inverse-mirror embodies the exact opposite (timid vs reckless, cruel vs altruistic). This is the RPG application of the literary "foil": two characters in whom one's strength and weakness are reflected and inverted in the other. A foil need not be an antagonist: it can be an ally, a loved one, or a rival who, through juxtaposition, reveals who the PC really is.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM starts from the existing sheets/PCs and not from an abstract NPC concept. Concrete steps: 1) Identify for each PC their central stake (dilemma, value, fear, ambition) — often already supplied by the sheet (background, tie, debt, secret). 2) Choose an axis of reflection: either resemblance (the NPC has the same problem but has solved it in a way the PC refuses or dreads) or inversion (the NPC embodies the opposite trait). 3) Write the NPC as one sentence of explicit opposition: "like X, but who chose Y." 4) Plug them into the situation via a relationship map (R-Map) so that they need something from the PC, which guarantees the collision. 5) Leave blanks (the "draw maps, leave blanks" principle): do not fix their reactions, only their axis of contrast, so as to adjust in play. Prep tip: if there are several PCs, a single NPC can reflect one and invert another, which creates opposing readings of the same character at the table.

**Variants.**
- Resemblance-mirror: the NPC shares the PC's dilemma but has resolved it — they show what the PC could become (warning or temptation)
- Inverse-mirror / antithesis: the NPC embodies the opposite trait to bring out the PC's by contrast
- Dark double / Gothic double: a corrupted or fallen version of the PC (Bizarro, evil version, alternate self)
- Relational mirror: an NPC who reflects not a single PC but their relationship with another (e.g. an NPC couple that foreshadows the fate of a PC duo)
- Evolving mirror: starts as a faithful reflection then diverges in play according to the PC's choices, underscoring the distance traveled
- Collective mirror: the NPC reflects one PC and inverts another, becoming loved and hated depending on the players

**System-agnostic:** yes — A narrative technique transferable to any system, including simulationist (a game like Zombiology can give the PC a survivor-NPC who made the opposite choices in the face of infection). It takes on particular salience in PbtA / relational drama (Monsterhearts, Apocalypse World) where the PC sheets explicitly expose stakes, ties, and conditions of growth, directly supplying the material for the reflection; but it is not exclusive to them. In sim games, the reflection expresses itself more through tactical/moral choices facing a shared environment than through an imposed theme.

**Forge equivalent.** In the Forge vocabulary: the relationship map (R-Map) of Sorcerer combined with the literary foil. The mirror NPC is typically an R-Map node that "needs something from the PC" and that addresses the same Premise as the protagonist — it serves as a source of Bangs (situations that demand a decision from the PC and reveal their character). The reflection/inversion is the way to guarantee that the NPC tests precisely the PC's thematic stake rather than a generic conflict.

**Sources:** [Foil (narrative) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foil_(narrative)) · [What Is a Foil Character in Literature? — MasterClass](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/writing-101-what-is-a-foil-character-in-literature-learn-about-2-types-of-literary-foils-and-the-differences-between-foil-and-antagonist) · [The Forge :: Narrativism: Story Now (Bangs, R-Maps, Premise) — Ron Edwards](http://www.indie-rpgs.com/_articles/narr_essay.html)

## Giving poisoned gifts (offering the PC what they want with a hidden cost)

**Definition.** A GMing technique consisting of giving a character what they covet (power, information, ally, object, victory) while attaching to it a cost, a debt, or a consequence — either explicit and negotiated, or concealed and deferred. The gift advances the PC toward their desire but burdens their situation: it creates dramatic tension by turning an apparent gain into narrative leverage. Two main flavors: the transparent bargain that the player accepts knowingly ("I give you X, but Y will happen"), and the truly poisoned gift whose price is revealed only after the fact.

**In preparation.** In preparation (and not at the table by improvisation), proceed as follows: (1) For each PC, explicitly list what they WANT — stated goal, secret desire, missing resource, coveted relationship. This is the raw material of the gift. (2) For each of these desires, pre-write one or two plausible "costs" anchored in the fiction: a debt to a faction, a clock/threat that starts ticking, a compromising secret, the hostility of an NPC, the loss of another cherished thing. (3) Designate the bearer of the gift: an NPC with their own motivations (a patron, a demon, an ambiguous mentor) makes the cost credible and exploitable later. (4) Prepare the deferred trigger: note where, when, and how the cost will resurface (who reclaims their due, in which session). For the "transparent bargain" mode, prepare several offers ready to deploy; for the "hidden" mode, never lie about the fiction — sow the clues upstream that the player could have read. (5) Calibrate: aim for a cost tempting enough to be taken, painful enough to count (practical benchmark: players accept about half the bargains offered).

**Variants.**
- Explicit Devil's Bargain (Blades in the Dark): the cost is announced and negotiated, the player freely chooses to accept it in exchange for a bonus or a success
- Truly poisoned gift: the price is concealed and only revealed in use or later (cursed object, traitorous ally, pact with fine print)
- Immediate cost vs. deferred cost: pay now (resource loss, wound) or later (clock, debt, future vengeance)
- Gift that creates a relational obligation: one now owes a favor to a powerful NPC or faction
- Gift that solves one problem by creating a bigger one (escalation): the solution draws a greater threat
- Moral temptation: the gift forces a choice between the character's desire and their values or ties
- Collective poisoned gift: the gain benefits the group but the cost falls on a single PC, creating internal tension

**System-agnostic:** yes — Transferable to any system, including simulationist (e.g. Zombiology): no dedicated mechanic is needed, it suffices that the fiction carry a desire and a cost. The version FORMALIZED in rules, however, is specific to two families. In PbtA (Apocalypse World, Dungeon World), "Offer an opportunity, with or without a cost" is a codified GM move, triggered when the players wait to learn what happens; the cost there is a free narrative tool. In Blades in the Dark (and Forged in the Dark heirs), the "Devil's Bargain" is a structured mechanic: an offer made before a roll, adding a die in exchange for an accepted complication. In a sim game, you keep the spirit (NPC-bearer, cost anchored in the fiction and applied via the existing rules: resources, health, reputation) without the bonus mechanic.

**Forge equivalent.** Falls under the "Bang" (Ron Edwards): a prepared situation that forces a character-revealing decision — the poisoned gift is a Bang with a costly choice. It also overlaps with the notion of "Reward as bait / addressing Premise": rewarding the player while relaunching the thematic pressure. On the Forge agenda side, it is a typically Narrativist way of "putting pressure on the character's Premise" by trading their desires against their values.

**Sources:** [Dungeon World SRD — Gamemastering (GM Move : Offer an opportunity, with or without cost)](https://www.dungeonworldsrd.com/gamemastering/) · [A Mad Lib For Your Devil's Bargains — Roleplaying Tips](https://www.roleplayingtips.com/adventure-building-campaigns/a-mad-lib-for-your-devils-bargains/) · [Devil's Bargains (Blades in the Dark) with Pam Punzalan — Dice Exploder](https://diceexploder.substack.com/p/devils-bargains-blades-in-the-dark)

## Bangs (provocative situations forcing a PC's choice) — Sorcerer / Ron Edwards

**Definition.** A Bang is a discrete, "explosive" event or circumstance that the character must confront, and that demands of the player a decision about how to react — a decision that must matter in an interesting and emotionally engaging way. The central criterion: a true Bang offers options and requires a choice without dictating the answer; even inaction must have consequences. The Bang reveals and develops the character by posing a question (the Premise) to which the player answers through their choice. Special case: the Kicker, a major and upheaving Bang defined by the player at character creation, which kicks off the fiction at the moment play begins.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM builds neither plot nor denouement: they prepare a situation (NPCs, stakes, relationships) then a "bandolier of bangs" — a bandolier of Bangs ready to be pulled. Concretely: 1) Build the situation AFTER character creation, anchoring it in their Kickers, ties, and goals; 2) List a handful of Bangs per session, each written in a sentence or two maximum (half-formed "bullets," not scripted scenes), so they can be adjusted or thrown out in play; 3) Formulate each Bang as a trigger for a choice, not as an imposed outcome — "learn that the princess is a prisoner" (provokes a choice) rather than "save the princess" (imposes the answer); "hear the wolf howl" rather than "kill the wolf"; 4) Verify that each response option engages the character's Premise/theme; 5) Organize the triggering via a timeline that separates background events from Bangs that directly touch the PCs, so as to know when to "fire" each Bang; 6) Stay ready to improvise: a Bang can also emerge from another player or from the PC themselves. Reuse tip: to adapt a commercial scenario, link its trigger Bangs directly to the PCs' interests and ties.

**Variants.**
- Kicker: an initial and upheaving Bang defined by the player at creation, the mandatory starting point of the character's fiction
- Bandolier of bangs: a prepared stock of several Bangs per session, disposable and modular
- Background Bang vs PC-targeted Bang: a background event (the world turns) as opposed to a Bang that personally forces a PC to choose
- Emergent Bang: unprepared, identified in play by another player or by the PC themselves as a moment of choice
- Escalating Bang: prepared as a reaction ready to intensify the pressure if the players stall

**System-agnostic:** yes — The Bang is conceptually narrativist (from Sorcerer / the Forge's GNS theory), but as a preparation technique it is transferable to any system, including a simulation game like Zombiology: preparing situations forcing morally/emotionally costly choices requires no particular mechanic. The nuance: in a sim/gamist game, the Bang will work mainly as a pacing tool and a dramatic dilemma grafted onto the game's own stakes, without necessarily aiming for a single thematic Premise as in the original narrativist framework.

**Forge equivalent.** A concept native to the Forge: the Bang is directly tied to the Premise and to "Story Now" (Narrativism). In Ron Edwards's essay, all the elements supplied by the GM via Bangs serve to advance the fiction from decision to decision, each choice proposing an answer to the moral/thematic question (Premise). The Kicker is its inaugural case; neighbor of the "Flag" (signal of player interest) and of "Address the Premise."

**Sources:** [The Alexandrian — The Art of Pacing: Prepping Bangs](https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/36768/roleplaying-games/the-art-of-pacing-prepping-bangs) · [Sorcerer (role-playing game) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sorcerer_(role-playing_game)) · [The Forge — Narrativism: Story Now (Ron Edwards)](http://www.indie-rpgs.com/_articles/narr_essay.html)

## Kicker (a player-authored triggering event, Sorcerer)

**Definition.** The Kicker is an upheaving event that occurs just before play begins and that turns the character's life upside down, triggering their personal arc. Its fundamental characteristic, introduced by Ron Edwards in Sorcerer (1996/2001), is that it is written by the PLAYER, not the GM, during the last step of character creation. A good Kicker presents a conflict, a threat, or an opportunity of strong personal significance, demands a reaction (the character cannot ignore it), opens a range of possible actions, but never dictates how the character must react. Three main forms are distinguished: the shock (brutal revelation), the opportunity, and the mystery.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM does NOT create the Kicker — they receive it from the players. Their job consists of exploiting these Kickers to build the starting situation. Concretely: (1) Collect the Kickers of each PC at the end of character creation. (2) Map what each Kicker implies: who is involved (NPCs, demonic parents, factions), what stake, what places. (3) Weave the individual Kickers together into a coherent situation — look for overlaps (a shared NPC, a shared place, interests that cross or oppose) so that the personal arcs collide. (4) Prepare the "relationship map" around the elements raised by the Kickers, rather than a linear scenario. (5) Derive the first Bangs (explosive on-stage situations) directly from the Kickers: the Kicker supplies the first dramatic pressure, the GM prepares the confrontations that force choices. (6) Do not resolve the Kicker in advance or decide its outcome — prepare forces and actors that react, let the meaning reveal itself in play. In short, the Kicker transforms GM prep: you prepare reactive situations around the players' opening choices, not a pre-established plot.

**Variants.**
- Belief / Drive (Burning Wheel): beliefs and instincts written by the player that the GM must challenge — same logic of player-authored seeding, but continuous rather than one-shot.
- Background/Bonds/Flags (PbtA, Apocalypse World, Monsterhearts): ties and relational facts written by the players that orient what the GM aims at — Kicker diffused through the relational fabric rather than concentrated in a single event.
- Player-authored Quests/Aspirations (D&D 4e, FATE aspects): declared personal goals that guide the GM's prep.
- Collective / group Kicker: a single triggering event co-written for the whole table, useful in one-shots or for a channeled start.
- Deferred / 'soft' Kicker: the player fixes the event but deliberately leaves its meaning open, the GM and player discovering it in play.
- Guided session zero: the GM proposes seeds, the players turn them into personal Kickers — a GM/player hybrid.

**System-agnostic:** yes — Originating in Sorcerer/Forge, the Kicker is transferable to any system, including a simulationist game like Zombiology: ask each player to write, at the end of creation, a personal event that forces their character to act, then build the starting situation around these seeds. No particular mechanic is required — it is a technique of fictional framing and distribution of narrative authority. Caveat: it serves mainly games that value personal arcs and reactivity; in a very scripted/railroad framework it conflicts with the pre-written plot.

**Forge equivalent.** Bang (Sorcerer / Forge lexicon): whereas the Kicker is the player-authored arc-seed upstream of play, the Bang is the charged situation prepared by the GM and dropped on stage to force a dramatic choice. The Kicker is also a particular case of the Forge notion of shared authority / 'player as author of premise' and feeds the 'Relationship Map' that structures situational rather than narrative prep.

**Sources:** [Here's the Kicker: Character creation and plot hooks — Step Into RPGs](https://stepintorpgs.wordpress.com/2015/04/25/heres-the-kicker-character-creation-and-plot-hooks/) · [The Historical Importance of Ron Edwards' Sorcerer — EN World](https://www.enworld.org/threads/the-historical-importance-of-ron-edwards-sorcerer.695809/) · [The Art of Pacing: Prepping Bangs — The Alexandrian](https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/36768/roleplaying-games/the-art-of-pacing-prepping-bangs)

## Flags (player signals that prep must aim at)

**Definition.** A Flag is an explicit element of the character sheet by which the player declares to the GM and the table the kind of conflicts, stakes, or narrative focus they want tested for their character. The term (popularized by Chris Chinn / bankuei in the Forge movement) refers to the idea of "planting a flag": marking a spot the game should aim at. Discriminating criterion: if the GM has to ask the player why they chose a given trait, it is not a Flag. A Flag is an explicit declaration of intent, not a statistic to interpret (a high combat score is NOT a Flag). Four families: Goals (vengeance, rebuild a world), Relationships (protect a loved one, a tense tie), Ideals (honor, loyalty), and inner Doubts/Fears. Good Flags: real player buy-in, uncertain outcome (nothing untouchable), and revisable during play. System examples: Spiritual Attributes (The Riddle of Steel), Beliefs (Burning Wheel), Keys (The Shadow of Yesterday), Aspects (Fate), agenda/Strings (PbtA), Conditions/Sex Moves in Monsterhearts.

**In preparation.** The "Flag Framing" method of Chris Chinn for preparing a campaign: 1) First present the initial situation and a few key NPCs BEFORE finalizing the sheets. 2) Have each player write their Flags during character creation (1-2 sentence descriptions: a goal, a relationship, an ideal, or a fear that the character is willing to risk / for which they would cross a moral line). 3) Collect these Flags, then flesh out / add NPCs by giving them motivations and Flags that CROSS those of the PCs (the NPC threatens, embodies, or tests a PC's Flag). 4) Keep two simple lists: PCs + their Flags, and NPCs + their motivations/Flags; materialize the crossings via index cards, a relationship map, or a "conflict web." 5) DO NOT write scenes or a linear plot in advance: prep consists of loading the situation with pressures directed at the Flags, then improvising by framing scenes on the hottest Flag. Prep therefore aims at "what to put under pressure" rather than "what will happen." Reward loop: rewarding (mechanically and narratively) the pursuit/confrontation of Flags signals to the whole table where the salt of the game lies.

**Variants.**
- Goals: a goal the character pursues, whose tension arises from the price to be paid ('how far will you go?')
- Relationships: ties threatenable by loss of trust, resentment, misunderstanding
- Ideals: values (honor, loyalty) generating dilemmas of priority
- Self-doubt / Fear: inner conflicts over identity or ability, drivers of character arc
- Explicit mechanized Flags (Beliefs/Keys/Spiritual Attributes) vs informal Flags noted separately by the GM when the system provides none
- NPC Flags: give antagonists Flags that cross those of the PCs to automatically generate conflict
- Group / table Flags (shared theme or stake) in addition to individual Flags

**System-agnostic:** yes — Origin: indie narrativist / Forge design (Riddle of Steel, Burning Wheel, The Shadow of Yesterday) and formalization by Chris Chinn (bankuei). The technique is, however, presented as transferable: you can graft Flags onto any system, including a simulationist game like Zombiology, without touching the existing rules — it suffices that the GM solicit and note explicit declarations of intent from the players, then orient their prep on them. PbtA and Fate games merely have the advantage of providing mechanized and rewarded Flags by default.

**Forge equivalent.** Directly from the Forge vocabulary. Equivalents/relatives: "Flags" and "Flag Framing" (Chris Chinn); the "Bangs" of Sorcerer (Ron Edwards) — prepared situations that force a choice tied to the character's stakes; the "Reward Cycle" of the Big Model; and the notion of "Addressing Premise" in Narrativism: Story Now (Edwards), where prep must pose moral questions that the players' Flags designate.

**Sources:** [Flag Framing 1: Setting up a Campaign — Deeper in the Game (Chris Chinn)](https://bankuei.wordpress.com/2015/01/07/flag-framing-1-setting-up-a-campaign/) · [Making Good Flags — Deeper in the Game (Chris Chinn)](https://bankuei.wordpress.com/2013/11/19/making-good-flags/) · [Flags: A critical misunderstanding — Deeper in the Game (Chris Chinn)](https://bankuei.wordpress.com/2014/07/31/flags-a-critical-misunderstanding/)

## The Czege Principle (don't be the sole author of both the conflict and its resolution)

**Definition.** A principle of RPG theory formulated by Paul Czege (and named by Ron Edwards on The Forge, from a comment made to Vincent Baker): "When one person is the author of both a character's adversity AND its resolution, the game is not fun." It distinguishes two narrative functions that are best not combined in a single head: the "author of adversity" (who invents the problem/conflict/obstacle) and the "author of resolution" (who decides how the character gets out of it). The dramatic interest arises from uncertainty: if I create the danger AND its outcome, there is neither stake nor surprise, just a story I tell myself.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM does NOT treat the principle as "I must decide nothing," but as "I must build a separation of the two functions." Concretely: (1) Prepare the adversity (NPCs with their own agendas, fronts, threats, situations under tension) WITHOUT pre-writing how the PCs will resolve it — no single scripted solution. You prepare problems, not denouements. (2) If the GM is going to be both the author of adversity and the arbiter, delegate the resolution to the system: prepare resolution mechanics (rolls, tables, conditions for success/failure) that decide in their place, so they are not the sole judge of the outcome. (3) Give the players the authority of resolution: provide levers (resources, choices, declarations) by which THEY decide how to confront what is set against them. (4) For a GM who prepares a scenario they will run themselves, the trap to avoid is the "railroad": preparing the obstacle AND its planned solution. The principle pushes one to prepare open situations and to let chance + players co-determine the exit. In solo (preparing oracles, tables, decks), the whole point of prep is to install a source of adversity external to the player: a random oracle, an event table, a card draw, systemic constraints, so that it is not the player who self-assigns both the obstacles AND their resolution.

**Variants.**
- Classic GM/players separation: the GM is the author of adversity, the players (via their characters and choices) are the authors of resolution.
- Delegation to the system/chance: when a single person carries both roles, the resolution (or the adversity) is entrusted to dice, tables, or neutral mechanics.
- Oracles in solo play: an oracle or a deck generates the adversity and/or the interpretation, removing total control of the outcome from the solo player.
- Rotating sharing of narrative authority (GM-less / structured freeform): the players assign complications to one another, no one resolves their own conflict.
- Critique/transcendence: some designers (e.g. Avery Alder) consider that successful solo games show the principle is not an absolute law but a heuristic — one can knowingly author both if another source of tension (emotional uncertainty, writing constraint, mechanical surprise) takes over.

**System-agnostic:** yes — The principle comes from the Forge / narrativist movement but it is system-agnostic: it describes a dynamic of narrative authority valid for any game, including simulationist. In a sim game like Zombiology, it is respected naturally: the GM poses the threat (the horde, the epidemic, the situation), but the resolution is arbitrated by the rules and the players' choices, not decided in advance by the GM — that is precisely the separation the principle recommends. The typical sim risk is the GM who "decides" the outcome despite the dice; the principle serves as a warning against that. No PbtA system is needed to apply it.

**Forge equivalent.** Very central in the Forge corpus: tied to the notions of Author Stance / Director Stance, to the distribution of narrative "authority" (Content/Plot/Situation authority), and to Ron Edwards's "Story Now" Narrativism. It is also the theoretical foundation of the GM/players "asymmetry" and of designs where the system (and not a person) arbitrates the adversity. Discussed in depth on Vincent Baker's anyway. blog (lumpley.com), who helped formalize author of adversity vs author of resolution.

## Fronts and clocks / countdown clocks (Apocalypse World, Dungeon World) as a preparation tool

**Definition.** A Front is a structured collection of linked threats (the "dangers") aimed at the PCs and the things they care about, each endowed with an "impulse" (its own motivation that drives it to act on its own) and an impending doom. The "grim portents" (Dungeon World) or the clocks/countdown clocks (Apocalypse World) break down the progression of each danger into visible stages describing what will happen if nothing stops it. The tool serves the GM in preparing a dynamic situation rather than a scripted plot: you prepare forces in motion, then "play to find out" how they evolve on contact with the PCs. The clock is a reminder signaling that the threats have a direction, a plan, and the will to act coherently.

**In preparation.** In preparation (ideally after the first session, once the PCs and the setting are known): 1) Choose the type of front — campaign front (slow, simmering in the background) or adventure front (episodic, 1 to 3 sessions). 2) Define 2 to 3 dangers, each with a name, a type (ambitious organization, planar/supernatural force, cursed place, lone monster, etc.), an impulse (its crucial motivation), and a cast of associated NPCs. 3) Assign each danger an impending doom (tyranny, plague, destruction, usurpation, impoverishment, unleashed chaos…). 4) Write the grim portents: 1 to 3 for an adventure front, 3 to 5 for a campaign front — the concrete, visible stages the danger will cross if it is not countered, from "bad" to "much worse." 5) Pose 1 to 3 stakes questions about NPCs/places whose fate you sincerely want to discover in play. 6) Optional: create custom moves specific to the front. In the clock version (Apocalypse World), you draw a dial (segments) per threat; you advance it either descriptively (you have seen the change happen) or prescriptively (you advance it as a hard move following a failure or an opportunity). You can strip the clock of its fine details and show it to the players to make the danger legible. Practical advice: maintain about three simultaneous fronts, at varied rates of progression, always leaving the PCs opportunities to push back the threat.

**Variants.**
- Ordered narrative grim portents (Dungeon World): stages in a logical chain, from bad to worse
- Segmented countdown clocks (Apocalypse World): a clock face, the first notches in quarter-hours then notches closer and closer together, with a point of no return (nothing after 21:00 is reversible, only mitigable)
- Progress/project clocks of Blades in the Dark / Forged in the Dark: generic 4/6/8-segment clocks to track any process, not only threats
- Simplified front à la SlyFlourish: major NPC + goal + lieutenant + enacting group, transposable to D&D and other classic games
- Descriptive advancement (you tick off what you have seen happen) vs prescriptive (you advance it as a GM hard move)
- Racing/opposing clocks: two competing clocks (the PCs vs the threat) filled in parallel
- 13th Age's icon rolls as a complementary mechanic to pace the progression of the fronts

**System-agnostic:** yes — The formalized vocabulary (fronts, dangers, impulses, grim portents, impending doom, moves) is native to the PbtA family (Apocalypse World, Dungeon World) and strongly integrated into its "play to find out" and "make a hard move when the opportunity presents itself" loop. However, the underlying principle — preparing antagonistic forces endowed with a trajectory in visible stages rather than a scripted plot — is entirely transferable. SlyFlourish explicitly demonstrates its use in D&D, and clocks (countdown/progress clocks) have become a generic tool adopted well beyond PbtA (Blades in the Dark, and usable in any sim game like Zombiology to pace an epidemic, the arrival of a horde, or the collapse of a shelter). In a sim system, the "prescriptive advancement tied to a failed move" is simply replaced by a fictional trigger or a roll of the host system.

**Forge equivalent.** Close to the "Bangs" and the situation preparation (Situation/Setup) of Ron Edwards (Sorcerer, Big Model): preparing elements charged with tension and relationships under pressure whose outcome you do not know, rather than a sequence. It also falls under "no myth / play to find out" play and Story Now: the GM poses forces in motion and stakes questions, and the narrative authority resolves itself during play rather than at preparation.

## Designing a character arc in RPG preparation

**Definition.** A character arc is the trajectory of inner transformation of a player character (PC) over the course of a campaign: a starting state (value, fear, belief, wound), a dramatic pressure that tests it, and a potential ending state. Three classic forms are distinguished: the change arc (the character transforms deeply), the growth arc (they strengthen inwardly, not only mechanically), and the failure/fall arc (they lose status, power, or integrity, sometimes to the point of becoming an antagonist). On the preparation side, designing an arc consists, for the GM, of identifying these axes from the PC's background, then pre-positioning in the world the NPCs, places, and dilemmas that may provoke the transformation — without deciding its outcome.

**In preparation.** In preparation (off-table), proceed as follows: 1) Gather material from the player: background, desires for transformation, planted "seeds" — the responsibility of articulating the wanted arc is shared between player and GM. 2) For each PC, formulate an arc sentence: "initial state -> tension -> possible outcome" (e.g. "coward who learns courage," "idealist who becomes corrupted"), and choose the type (change / growth / failure). 3) Isolate the thematic premise of the arc as an open question (e.g. "How far will he betray to protect his sister?") whose outcome is NOT decided in advance. 4) Identify in the background the re-activatable elements: related NPCs, places, an object, a debt, a secret. 5) Prepare a reserve of pressure situations — Ron Edwards's "bandolier of bangs": events provoking a thematically significant decision that directly touches the arc (bring back the traitorous mentor, set the PC's value in conflict with their survival). 6) Lay out these triggers as modifiable/disposable hooks, not as a script — the arc reveals itself in play, the GM does not resolve it alone. 7) Watch over the balance of spotlight time between PCs. 8) Think about the ending: provide moments where the PC can tip over (or refuse to tip over), because it is the player's choice that closes the arc.

**Variants.**
- Change arc: deep transformation of the character's nature (the coward becomes brave)
- Growth arc: progressive inner strengthening, the most common for a PC; avoid evolving only the numbers on the sheet
- Failure / fall arc (negative/fall arc): loss of power, status, or integrity, possible tipping into the status of antagonist; requires a long build-up
- Flat arc (flat/testing arc): the character does not change but their conviction is tested and confirmed, and they transform the world around them
- Emergent approach (PbtA / Forge style): do not write the arc in advance but prepare bangs and let the arc emerge from the choices in play
- Backstory-driven approach (D&D / GM-player duo): GM and player co-define the arc from the bonds/flaws/ideals and the GM weaves NPCs and scenes accordingly

**System-agnostic:** yes — Designing an arc in preparation is transferable to any system, including a predominantly simulationist game like Zombiology: the arc there becomes a dramatic thread added on top of the sim (e.g. a survivor sinking into paranoia), provoked by pressure situations rather than by dedicated rules. Nuance: PbtA and Forge-derived games (Sorcerer, Apocalypse World) integrate the arc into their very procedures — initial Kicker, fronts/threats, the "play to find out what happens" principle — so that the arc emerges more from play than from pre-written preparation. In a classic/sim game, the GM must on the contrary actively prepare the triggers because the system does not generate them.

**Forge equivalent.** In the Forge corpus (Ron Edwards), the preparation equivalent is the Kicker / Bang pair. The Kicker (Sorcerer) is the starting event, chosen by the player, that propels the character into the action and seeds their arc. The Bang is the event introduced by the GM to force a thematically significant choice; the GM prepares a reserve of them (the "bandolier of bangs") and builds the scenario AFTER character creation, from the characters. The Premise (narrativist premise: an open moral/thematic question) is what the arc puts into play, the outcome remaining undecided — the GM provokes via Bangs without resorting to Force (an imposed outcome).

## Diversifying and opposing the characters' goals (contradictory goals)

**Definition.** A dramatic framing technique consisting of endowing each character (PCs and major NPCs) with distinct goals that, by design, overlap and come into tension. Rather than a united team pursuing a common goal, the group is crossed by incompatible desires: what one wants threatens what another wants. The engine of fiction is no longer the external obstacle but the friction between agendas. This is sometimes called "triangulation" (a single NPC is the ally of one and the enemy of another) or situations where satisfying one goal costs something to another character.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM does not prepare a plot but a CONFIGURATION of crossed goals. Concretely: 1) List for each PC their main desire/agenda (from the sheet, the established relationships, the background). 2) List the key NPCs and give each one an explicit and active goal. 3) Draw a relationship map / "triangles": for each NPC, identify at least one PC for whom they are an asset and another for whom they are a threat; for each resource or stake, verify that at least two characters covet it in a mutually exclusive way. 4) Forge "bangs" (Ron Edwards): seed situations where a character must choose now between two things they want, or between their goal and an ally's. 5) Do not over-prepare the resolution: you prepare the tension (who wants what, and why it cannot coexist), not the denouement, which emerges in play. In a fronts logic (PbtA), note "portents": what will happen if a given goal is not countered, to keep the agendas in motion even off-screen.

**Variants.**
- Triangulation: each key NPC is the friend of one PC and the enemy of another, guaranteeing that every relational choice divides the group.
- Single rival resource: a single object/post/person desired that several characters cannot obtain simultaneously.
- Conflict of ideologies rather than goals: same facts, incompatible values about how to handle them (useful to avoid open betrayal that breaks the table).
- Distributed secret goals: each player privately receives a goal that rubs against the others' (close to hidden-goal play).
- NPC agendas beyond combat: the adversary does not want to kill but to extort, convert, seduce — which forces dramatic trade-offs.
- Divergent scales: align the PCs on the same side but at different ranges (intimate vs communal vs global), which makes views contradictory without breaking the group (Apocalypse World model).

**System-agnostic:** yes — Transferable to any system, including simulationist (e.g. Zombiology): the technique operates at the level of situation and motivation preparation, independently of mechanical resolution. It is merely more *tooled* in narrativist/PbtA games, where relationships (Hx/strings), fronts, relationship moves, and the XP system actively push the characters to grow closer while remaining at odds. In a sim game, the GM achieves the same effect by hand by positing persistent NPC agendas and rival stakes, without dedicated mechanical support. Cross-cutting caution: keep the contradictory goals *within* cooperation (rivalry, divergence of values) rather than pure betrayal, which tends to destroy the play group.

**Forge equivalent.** The "Bangs" of Ron Edwards (Sorcerer): prepared situations where a character endowed with multiple desires is set in conflict with themselves or with others and must act now. Tied to the narrativist "Premise" (addressing a thematic question through the fiction, whose resolution arises from the conflict between protagonists). On the PbtA side (heir to the Forge via Vincent Baker), the equivalent is the design of relationships and fronts so that the characters are "at odds, but working together" — each NPC being the ally of one and the enemy of another.

## Interweaving the plots (several crossed narrative threads)

**Definition.** A narrative-structuring technique consisting of advancing in parallel several distinct plot threads (each with its own motivation, its NPCs, and its resolution) within a single campaign, making them overlap, influence one another, and converge rather than handling them one after the other. "Braiding" alternates the spotlight between the threads session after session or scene after scene, which maintains tension, avoids weariness, and creates the impression of a living and coherent world.

**In preparation.** In preparation: 1) List 3 to 5 active arcs/threads (a major arc that closes the campaign, 1-2 minor arcs, possibly an episodic "one-shot" thread), anchoring at least some threads in the PCs' backstories and goals. 2) Build a braiding grid: a table where each COLUMN is a thread and each ROW a moment of play; each cell contains just enough material (1 to 3 paragraphs, 2-4 cells per page) to advance that thread before handing back. 3) Decide the temporal relationships between threads: simultaneous, sequential, overlapping, or with intervals (justified by travel/delay). 4) Highlight the names of NPCs/PCs in each cell to spot impossibilities (the same character in two places) and verify that the "spotlight time" is balanced between threads. 5) Provide crossing points: an NPC, an object, or a place that belongs to two threads and makes them collide; plant interlocking clues/secrets (each revelation opens another mystery). 6) Keep the transitions/segues detailed the first time (place, participants), shorter on subsequent returns. Consistency tip via retroactive cohesion: reserve the possibility of linking old events after the fact (a "minor" antagonist becomes the cause of a later event) to give an impression of overall design.

**Variants.**
- Braiding in a table: columns = threads, rows = beats, to drive the alternation of the spotlight
- A-plot / B-plot / C-plot model: a main thread and competing secondary threads that take turns
- 'Godzilla' rhythm: a thread sleeps for a long time then explodes in a few intense sessions before going back to sleep
- 'Gray Goo' rhythm: a thread progressively eats up more and more sessions until it dominates the campaign
- Relationship map: you interweave the threads via a network of linked NPCs rather than via predefined arcs — the crossings emerge from play
- Terminal convergence: all the threads are designed to come together at a common climax
- Retroactive braiding: you knot independent events together after the fact to give them meaning

**System-agnostic:** yes — Transferable to any system, including simulationist (e.g. Zombiology). Nuance: in games with strong emergent narrative structure (PbtA, Forge/Sorcerer), the interweaving is prepared less as a grid of fixed arcs and more as a relationship map + "bangs" whose crossings emerge in play; in a sim/sandbox game, the threads are often autonomous dynamics (factions, clocks, threats) that cross by geography and time rather than by an author's plan.

**Forge equivalent.** The relationship map of Sorcerer / 'The Sorcerer's Soul' (Ron Edwards), coupled with the 'Weavings' introduced in Sex & Sorcery: a network of linked NPCs generates plot crossings, and the Bangs pushed toward the PCs naturally make their respective threads interweave instead of a pre-scripted plot.

**Sources:** [The Braiding Of Plot Threads — Campaign Mastery](https://www.campaignmastery.com/blog/the-braiding-of-plot-threads/) · [A Plot, B Plot; One, Two, Three Plot (An Ask Angry Special) — The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/a-plot-b-plot/) · [The Provisional Glossary (Bangs, Scene Framing, Relationship Map) — The Forge](http://www.indie-rpgs.com/_articles/glossary.html)

## Generating complex relationships between NPCs (ambivalent ties)

**Definition.** A preparation technique consisting of designing a network of NPCs connected not by simple labels (ally/enemy), but by ties charged with ambivalence: a single tie combines contradictory forces (love + resentment, loyalty + latent betrayal, debt + rivalry). Each NPC carries their own desire and an object of opposition, so that the dramatic pressure pre-exists the PCs' arrival and triggers as soon as they touch a thread of the network. Formalized notably in the relationship map of Sorcerer (Ron Edwards) and popularized by Hillfolk/DramaSystem-style relationship maps.

**In preparation.** In preparation (and not in direct play): 1) List 6 to 10 NPCs and lay them out as nodes on a sheet/canvas (Obsidian Portal, spreadsheet, paper). 2) Draw a line between each connected pair and inscribe on it NOT a neutral label but a doubly-charged tie: indicate what unites them AND what eats at them ("his sister — whom he protects and whose inheritance he covets"). 3) For each NPC, note a desire/best interest (what they want) and a vector of opposition (who or what blocks their path) — in the manner of Sorcerer/In a Wicked Age. 4) Apply a density rule: aim for at least 2-3 ties per NPC and spot the "canary in the coal mine" (any node with a single tie is weakly integrated, so reconnect or cut it). 5) Plug in the PCs: have each PC enter through at least one thread of the network (a personal tie, a debt, a shared secret). 6) Convert the latent frictions into bangs ready to deploy: for each point where two desires collide, formulate an explosive situation ("when X discovers Y, they will force the PCs to choose"). 7) Give the network a past and a trajectory (where it was heading before the PCs), and redraw/revise the map between sessions as the ties evolve.

**Variants.**
- Visual relationship map (R-map) à la Sorcerer/Hillfolk: nodes + annotated edges, redrawn each session as a prep ritual
- Co-creation at the table: ask the players to invent an NPC or a faction tied to their character, then integrate (or reinterpret) their answers — gains buy-in and effort
- Vectorized ties (In a Wicked Age): best interests + vectors of opposition, without specifying the emotion, letting the ambivalence emerge in play
- The minimum-three-connections rule: force each NPC to at least three ties for a social network that reacts naturally to the PCs' actions
- Romantic/loyalty triangulation: organize triangles (A loves B who depends on C who hates A) rather than dyads, multiplying the tensions
- Literary extraction: take the relationship map of a noir novel (Hammett) or a tragedy and substitute the PCs as the entering protagonists

**System-agnostic:** yes — Transferable to any system, including simulationist (a game like Zombiology): the relationship map is a preparation artifact independent of mechanical resolution. In PbtA/narrativist games (Monsterhearts with its Strings, Apocalypse World with its Hx/fronts), the technique is partially formalized by the rules (numbered strings/ties, threats); in a sim or classic game, the GM applies it as an informal prep tool without mechanical support. The value (pre-existing dramatic pressure, NPCs with conflicting motivations) remains whatever the engine.

**Forge equivalent.** The relationship map (R-map) of Ron Edwards's Sorcerer: a web of NPCs with crossed desires and oppositions, endowed with a past and a trajectory, into which the PCs "enter like a protagonist of Dashiell Hammett." Coupled with the prep of bangs (the "bandolier of bangs": explosive situations born of the NPCs' contradictory desires, deployed in play) and the kicker (the PC's own initial bang). A related concept in Vincent Baker (In a Wicked Age): NPCs' best interests and vectors of opposition.

**Sources:** [Relationship Maps — Story Games Codex (origine Sorcerer / the Forge)](http://sgcodex.wikidot.com/relationship-maps) · [Relationship Mapping — Gnome Stew (Phil Vecchione)](https://gnomestew.com/relationship-mapping/) · [My recent beau of roleplaying games, the Relationship Map — The Indie Game Reading Club](https://www.indiegamereadingclub.com/indie-game-reading-club/my-recent-beau-of-roleplaying-games-the-relationship-map-this-is-my-first-attempt-at-the-beast-second-really/) · [The Art of Pacing: Prepping Bangs — The Alexandrian (Justin Alexander)](https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/36768/roleplaying-games/the-art-of-pacing-prepping-bangs)

## Describing the setting as a set of signs (meaningful worldbuilding)

**Definition.** An approach to designing and describing the setting where each concrete detail delivered at the table (object, place, garment, ritual, ruin, mark on a body) is not neutral set-dressing but a sign: it points toward a larger reality (history, power, belief, conflict) and carries a thematic charge. The GM does not describe the setting exhaustively or encyclopedically; they select salient, meaning-laden elements from which the players can infer the rest (inferred worldbuilding / iceberg theory). The setting becomes legible "like a text": the signs refer to one another and express what the fiction is really about.

**In preparation.** In preparation, do not write an exhaustive bible but constitute a bank of signs. 1) First identify the theme(s)/tension(s) of the campaign (what the story is about: decline, control, faith, predation, etc.). 2) For each planned place, faction, or NPC, note only 2-3 concrete, sensory details that "say" that theme by metonymy: not "the city is poor" but "the guild signs have been taken down and serve as firewood." 3) Verify that each detail is a hook (it poses a question, suggests an off-screen, calls for an action) rather than a closed datum. 4) Prepare a coherent "palette": visual/sonic/material recurrences (a material, a color, a ritual gesture) that recur and weave the identity of the setting. 5) Explicitly leave blanks: what the signs imply but that prep does not fix, so as to be able to improvise or co-build it. Concretely, a place sheet becomes a list of 3-5 signs + the question each one poses, not a descriptive paragraph.

**Variants.**
- Inferred worldbuilding / iceberg: show only the tip (the sign) and let the submerged mass be deduced, without ever fully writing it
- Palette / recurring motif: choose a small set of key-signs (material, color, sound, ritual) that you repeat to sign the setting and create thematic resonance
- Sign-hook (front / threat): each meaningful detail is also a seed for a situation to exploit, in the manner of Apocalypse World fronts
- Diegetic clue-detail: use objects and places as clues that the players actively read and interpret (the setting is a riddle to decode)
- Interface semiotics (for games with components/cards): conventional visual signs legible without explanation, transposed to verbal description
- From the arbitrary to the motivated: take a color element placed at random and promote it to a structuring, meaning-laden element (a movement described by Ron Edwards)

**System-agnostic:** yes — A technique transferable to any system, including a simulation game like Zombiology: describing a scene of contamination through signs (a fridge barricaded from the inside, scratch marks on a hospital door) works whatever the rules engine. The PbtA family, however, offers a strong and explicit formulation of it — "Barf forth apocalyptica" replacing the generic principle "Sprinkle evocative details everywhere" in Apocalypse World: the details are not sprinkled at random but must all speak of the same thematic world. PbtA also articulates the signs with "fronts" and "threats," which turn each meaningful detail into a seed for a playable situation.

**Forge equivalent.** "Color" in Forge theory (Ron Edwards): one of the five components of Exploration (Character, Setting, Situation, System, Color), defined as the "affective set-dressing in imagined space" — the details that affectively color the imagined space. Meaningful worldbuilding corresponds precisely to the gesture, described by Edwards, of moving Color "from arbitrary to motivated": a detail that starts out decorative becomes a structuring, meaning-laden element. Also to be linked to Setting (the context) and Situation (Character + Setting), since well-chosen signs let the Setting be seen and seed the Situation.

## Leaving clues and secrets floating (seeding clues / hidden fronts)

**Definition.** A preparation technique consisting of sowing in the world fragments of information (clues, traces, rumors, documents) and latent, not-yet-revealed dangers ("fronts" or background threats), so that the players themselves reconstitute a hidden truth or discover an ongoing threat. The central idea: the GM does not prepare a single path but a layer of redundant clues and dormant elements, which "float" until the PCs encounter them and give them meaning. The reference robustness principle is Justin Alexander's "Three Clue Rule": for any conclusion you want the PCs to reach, provide at least three distinct clues leading to it.

**In preparation.** In preparation, proceed in four stages. (1) List the CONCLUSIONS or hidden truths the PCs could discover ("the mayor is a werewolf," "the mine is haunted by X"). (2) For each one, write at least three independent clues, of varied natures (a witness, a physical object/trace, a document, a deduction by skill), distributed across different places and among different NPCs — never all in the same place nor accessible by the same roll. (3) Prepare dormant threats ("fronts"): for each hostile force, note its impulse/motivation, 1 to 3 "portents" (escalation stages if nothing stops it) and the final catastrophe it aims at; limit to 2-3 dangers and leave blanks. (4) Before each session, write a dozen short "secrets" (tweet-length) that the PCs can run into: these are floating clues ready to be placed in the course of play, with no fixed location. Keep a checklist of revelations to track what has actually been understood. Keep these clues as a safety net, not a straitjacket: reward any creative approach from the players (permissive clue-finding).

**Variants.**
- Three Clue Rule (Alexander): at least three clues per conclusion, of different supports
- Node-Based Scenario Design / Inverted Three Clue Rule: clues linking nodes to one another for a non-linear investigation rather than a single thread
- Secrets & Clues (Lazy DM, Sly Flourish): a pre-session list of ~10 non-localized secrets, distributed according to the flow
- Fronts & grim portents (Dungeon World / PbtA): hidden threats that advance in the background and reveal themselves through signs
- Proactive clues: when the PCs stall, have the antagonist act or advance a portent to generate new clues
- Quantum / floating clue: a clue not assigned in advance that the GM places wherever the PCs look (to be handled sparingly to preserve agency)
- Avoid deliberate red herrings: the players' suspicions create enough of them naturally

**System-agnostic:** yes — The core (sowing redundant clues toward conclusions, and preparing background threats that escalate) is entirely transferable, including to a simulationist game like Zombiology: there you prepare the spread of the infection as a "front" with portents, and multiple physical clues/testimonies toward each truth. Only the vocabulary "Fronts / grim portents / impending doom" is specific to the PbtA family (Apocalypse World, Dungeon World); in a sim system you will speak of node-based scenarios, of the antagonist's event chronology, or of a crisis timeline, but the preparation mechanic is the same.

**Forge equivalent.** In the Forge corpus, the closest equivalent is Ron Edwards's Bangs / Kickers distinction (Sorcerer): "bangs" are charged situations prepared by the GM that force a significant choice, relatives of the portents/fronts that "float" until they are triggered. More broadly, this falls under the preparation of "situation" rather than "plot" (prepare situations, not plots): you prepare forces, stakes, and latent information, not a scripted plot — which aligns with "play to find out" and the refusal of railroading.

## Proposing scenes that matter — scene framing & stakes

**Definition.** Scene framing consists of cutting the "empty time" and opening each scene directly onto a moment that demands a decision. A well-framed scene is defined by three elements: a point of view/frame (where the action takes place, who is present), an agenda — the dramatic question the scene answers ("Will Billy take the heroin?") — and stakes: what the characters can gain or lose. The rule is to frame as close as possible to the decision point, to state or make the stakes legible from the outset, then to cut the scene as soon as its question is resolved or sufficiently complicated ("cut sooner rather than later"). The dramatic engine of the scene is the "bang" (Ron Edwards, Sorcerer): the event that forces a meaningful choice, where inaction too has consequences.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM does not script complete scenes: they prepare a "bandolier of bangs." Concretely: (1) List 4 to 8 possible bangs for the session, each in one to two sentences maximum — an event that puts a PC before a difficult choice (e.g. "the cop who stops them is wearing the victim's wedding ring"). (2) For each bang, note in one line the agenda/the question it poses and the stakes (what is at stake for whom). (3) Anchor the bangs in the characters' "flags" — skills, backgrounds, kickers defined at creation — to aim at what interests each player. (4) Keep the bangs "unloaded": they are modular ammunition, not an order of scenes; you decide in play where and how to trigger them, and you throw some out without regret. (5) Optional: a light timeline indicating when certain bangs would occur if the PCs do not act, to support the pacing. For each scene, mentally prepare the "frame" (where you cut to enter) and the one-sentence statement of the stakes, utterable in one breath.

**Variants.**
- Aggressive scene framing (My Life with Master): cut without mercy to the point of tension, skipping all preamble
- Bang / bandolier of bangs (Sorcerer, Ron Edwards): prepare a stock of choice-triggers rather than written scenes
- Cut-to-the-action / flashback (Blades in the Dark): start the scene in the middle of the action and reconstruct the context afterward
- State the stakes out loud at the start of the scene (players hate confusion, not spoilers)
- Collaborative stakes-setting: negotiate with the players what is at stake before resolving (narrative-conflict games)
- Framing by 'question' (the Alexandrian): formulate each scene as a closed question to resolve
- Escalating bangs: chain several bangs in a scene to raise the stakes

**System-agnostic:** yes — The basic technique (frame as close as possible to the choice, make the stakes explicit, cut early) is transferable to any system, including a simulation game like Zombiology: you can frame a survival scene on the crucial decision (ration or share the supplies?) rather than on the journeys. The most formalized versions — bang, kicker, flags, negotiated stakes — come from the Forge narrativist movement (Sorcerer, PbtA, Blades) and assume a game oriented toward dramatic choices; in a purely sim game, you keep the spirit (scenes that matter, clear stakes) without necessarily formalizing the bangs around thematic dilemmas.

**Forge equivalent.** The "bang" of Ron Edwards (Sorcerer) and its "bandolier of bangs," backed by the Premise and the "Story Now" of narrativism; related Forge concepts: Kicker (initial bang chosen by the player), Flags/Keys (signals of player interest used to target the bangs), and the watchword "Get to the bangs!".

## Creating a memorable big bad / antagonist (goal, method, flaw)

**Definition.** Designing a central antagonist around three inseparable pillars: a GOAL (what they deeply want and what motivates them from their own point of view — "no one is the villain of their own story"), a METHOD (the concrete plan, the resources, and the steps by which they pursue that goal, even off-screen), and a FLAW (vulnerability, fear, or blind spot that the PCs can discover and exploit). A memorable antagonist is not a block of stats to defeat but an acting force of the world: they actively pursue their goal as long as they are not stopped, which gives the campaign a direction and points of collision with the characters.

**In preparation.** IN PREPARATION, the GM proceeds in layers rather than in a fixed scenario. 1) Write the GOAL in one sentence, then ask "Why?" three or four times in a row to reach a motivation that everyone instantly understands (which makes their decisions coherent and predictable to themselves). 2) Deduce the METHOD: list what the antagonist does "if they are never countered" — a sequence of steps (3 resilient quests/milestones, without depending on a single branch), the necessary resources (minions, artifacts, places, allies), and their code of conduct (methodical or chaotic). 3) Define the FLAW in relation to their strengths: what do they fear, who can reach them emotionally, what blind spot. 4) Prepare the points of collision: how the PCs first discover the operations (investigation) before confronting them, and prepare contingencies (how they react when their plan is disturbed) rather than scripted combats. 5) Optional but powerful: hook a detail of the villain to the PCs' backstories to create a personal stake. The preparation thus produces a dynamic conflict structure, not a linear plot.

**Variants.**
- Clock tracking (PbtA, Forged in the Dark): materialize the advancement of the plan in segments; when the clock is filled, the antagonist's goal is realized — a tool of temporal pressure also usable outside PbtA
- Motivation twist: subvert the expectation (the barbarian king seeks worthy adversaries, not conquest) to add complexity
- Trope inversion: a big-hearted demon, a cold angel — defy stereotypes to make the antagonist striking
- Faction-antagonist rather than an individual: the "big bad" is an organization or a force, whose goal/method/flaw apply at the collective scale
- Flaw = unexpected mercy: a sensitive point from the past that can produce a surprising moment of clemency in play
- Antagonist without a mandatory final confrontation: too powerful to be killed but trappable, divertible, or circumventable

**System-agnostic:** yes — The goal/method/flaw triptych and writing the motivation through "Why?" are entirely transferable, including to a simulationist game like Zombiology (the antagonist there is designed identically, then resolved by the system's own mechanics). The "clock" variant and the notion of a Front were born in the PbtA / Forged in the Dark family (Apocalypse World, Dungeon World): the principle of temporal pressure is reusable everywhere, but the mechanical formalism (ticked segments, threat moves) is specific to these families.

**Forge equivalent.** The concept of the Front (Apocalypse World / Dungeon World), heir to the "Bangs" and to the notion of an NPC/threat with an agenda in Ron Edwards (Forge theory): the antagonist there is defined as a threat endowed with an Impulse/agenda that "advances" autonomously via the Fronts and the Grim Portents, which formalizes exactly the idea of a villain who actively pursues their goal off-screen. To be related also to the Forge principle "play to find out" / prep as situation not plot: you prepare a charged situation (the villain's goal and method) and not the outcome.

## Choosing a fitting narrative structure (vs the default structure)

**Definition.** Instead of systematically organizing a scenario as a linear sequence ("the default structure": scene A → scene B → scene C), the GM consciously chooses, during preparation, the form of organization that best matches the scenario's intention, their own comfort, and the desired degree of agency. The main forms described in the game-design literature are: linear (obstacle course, scenes imposed in order), branching (maze: decision points connecting interconnected scenes), open / nodal ("gelatinous cube": nodes — places, NPCs, events — accessible in any order via clues or choices), and hybrid (a combination of the three at different scales). The choice of structure is a deliberate act of design, not an automatism.

**In preparation.** In preparation, do not first write a chain of events ("A happens, then B"), but begin by diagnosing the scenario's intention and choosing the fitting form. Concrete steps: 1) Identify the goal of the scenario (investigation, exploration, relational drama, dungeon) and the degree of freedom desired for the players. 2) Choose the structure according to this goal AND your own GM strengths (Angry GM reminds us there is no "best" structure: a GM may excel at linear/branching and hate the open). 3) Prepare according to the chosen form: for a nodal structure (recommended for investigation by the Alexandrian), create interconnected nodes where each node contains clues pointing to others, rather than an imposed sequence; prepare the situation (who knows whom, who wants what, who is where) and not the unfolding. 4) Embrace hybridization: e.g. a pointcrawl for the movements, a site structure for a specific place, background events in parallel. 5) Verify robustness: if the players miss a clue or take another path, does the structure hold (a test the linear one fails)?

**Variants.**
- Linear / obstacle course: scenes imposed in order — quick to prepare, useful for a tutorial, a framed opening scene, or a climax
- Branching / maze: decision points connecting interconnected scenes — the most common structure (classic dungeon)
- Open / nodal: nodes accessible in any order via clues or choices (node-based design, west marches, pointcrawl) — ideal for investigation and exploration
- Hybrid / multi-scale: combine the three according to the zoom level (regional pointcrawl + site-based for a place + background events)
- Prepare the situation rather than the plot: the Forge relationship map — a network of NPCs with needs and tensions — as a non-sequential structural foundation

**System-agnostic:** yes — A technique transferable to any system, including simulationist (an investigation/survival game like Zombiology gains as much from a nodal structure as a PbtA does). However, some families encode a default structure in their procedures: PbtA games (Apocalypse World) push toward situation/fronts rather than plot, and the Forge's aggressive "scene framing" (My Life with Master, Blades in the Dark) almost imposes the open structure through scene framing. The principle of CHOOSING remains agnostic; it is the favored options that vary by family.

**Forge equivalent.** The Forge's "situation, not plot" pair (Ron Edwards): prepare a relationship map (a network of NPCs with conflicting needs) and Bangs (moments forcing the PCs to decide) rather than a sequential plot. Coupled with scene framing / "Get to the bangs!": the structure emerges from framing scenes toward the dramatic moments, and not from a pre-written sequence. It is the narrativist embodiment of "choosing a fitting structure": the nodal/open structure there is the direct equivalent of the default linear structure the technique invites you to abandon.

## Creating a scenario as a situation rather than a linear plot ("situation not plot," "Story Now")

**Definition.** An approach to scenario design that consists of preparing an unstable SITUATION — a set of circumstances, of characters with contradictory goals, and of stakes ready to explode — rather than a PLOT, that is, a predetermined sequence of events (A→B→C). A plot seeks to fix in advance what has not yet happened; a situation poses the conditions and lets the story emerge from the players' choices during play. This is the heart of "Story Now" theorized by Ron Edwards on The Forge: the story is produced DURING play (as opposed to "Story Before," where the unfolding is written beforehand). The GM does not plan the reactions, they react at the table.

**In preparation.** In preparation, you do NOT write a synopsis or a sequence of scenes. You fill a "toolbox": 1) the NPCs — their motivations, resources, relationships, without scripted dialogue; 2) the concrete places (where, what it looks like, what the PCs can do there); 3) for each antagonist, the question "what are they trying to do?" formulated as a goal or a timeline (what happens if the PCs do not intervene); 4) the untenable starting situation into which the characters will arrive, and its stakes; 5) "Bangs" à la Ron Edwards (Sorcerer) — prepared trigger-moments that force a moral/dramatic choice, kept as "bullets ready to be fired" whose timing you choose in play. You explicitly avoid "contingency planning" (anticipating a reaction for each possible PC action), which is in fact disguised plot preparation. Associated reliability tip: the three-clue rule (at least three paths toward any conclusion the PCs must reach).

**Variants.**
- Preparation by fronts / dangers (PbtA, Dungeon World, Apocalypse World): you list threats with their "grim portent" / countdown that advances off-screen
- Node-based scenario design (Alexandrian): elements connected by clues rather than by an imposed order
- "Problems, not plot": pose a problem to solve whose outcome is open
- Relationship map / kicker (Sorcerer): a relationship map + a personal hook that sets the PC in motion from the start
- Preparation by explicit Bangs: arrive at the session with a bandolier of strong moments ready to be pulled

**System-agnostic:** yes — The principle (situation rather than plot) is transferable to any system, including a simulationist game like Zombiology: you prepare the ecosystem (infection hotspots, factions and their goals, resources, worsening clocks) and let the consequences flow from the actions. The vocabulary differs by family: "Bangs"/"Story Now" come from Ron Edwards / The Forge (narrativism, Sorcerer, Trollbabe); "fronts"/"clocks" are the formalization of the same principle in the PbtA family. The underlying technique remains agnostic; only the shaping tools are specific to certain families.

**Forge equivalent.** "Story Now" (narrativism) in Ron Edwards: the situation prepares an "Address" of the moral/thematic Premise that the players' choices will settle in play. Associated concrete tool: the "Bang" (Sorcerer) — a point of situation forcing a significant decision. Forge distinction: Story Now (situation) vs Story Before (predetermined plot).

## Managing linearity: rail vs sandbox vs open situation

**Definition.** A spectrum of campaign structures describing the degree of predetermination of the PCs' path. The RAIL (linear) chains a scripted sequence of events (A→B→C) that the GM pushes the players to follow. The SANDBOX is an open and reactive world, with no imposed plot or goal, where the players are the drivers and the GM responds. The OPEN SITUATION is the middle way: you prepare a state of affairs (factions, places, NPCs with goals and resources, events that occur by default if the PCs do not intervene), but never the sequence of events, which flows from the PCs' choices. A key point from modern theory: "to railroad" is a verb (a GM method that negates the outcome produced by the choices and the mechanics), whereas sandbox/linear are campaign structures — a linear campaign is NOT necessarily railroading if the GM stays open to emergent consequences.

**In preparation.** In preparation, do NOT write a sequence of events or branches ("if the PCs do X then Y"), because any unused contingent prep is wasted. Instead, prepare a SITUATION in the form of a toolbox: 1) list the factions/antagonists with their goal, their resources, their personnel, and the information they hold; 2) map the relevant places with a coherent internal logic; 3) define a "baseline" — what will happen on its own if the PCs do nothing (e.g. "the villains flee on two ships toward Tharsis; by such-and-such hour they will be spotted by the navy"); 4) make sure each PC has their own motivation that pushes them to act without a hook; 5) distribute the information redundantly (Three Clue Rule) so the situation stays navigable whatever the path taken. You prepare reusable elements (the city, the wreck, the temple) that serve whatever the angle of approach, rather than a decision tree. Conscious slider: decide in advance what degree of linearity the table wants (cinematic one-shot = more rail; long campaign = more sandbox) and prepare accordingly.

**Variants.**
- Rail (linear): a scripted sequence — useful for one-shots, commercial scenarios, a focused cinematic experience, low need for improvisation
- Sandbox: an open reactive world — strong sense of agency, but risk of choice paralysis, demands a lot of prepared material and improvisation
- Open situation (prep situations, not plots — Justin Alexander): the middle ground, you prepare the state of affairs and the forces present, not the plot
- Hub and spoke: a stable central base from which autonomous branches depart; a common structural compromise
- Branching: several converging predetermined paths; more flexible than the pure rail but demands contingent prep
- Non-railroaded linear: a directed structure BUT with outcomes left open to choices and rolls — combines narrative focus and agency
- Illusionism / quantum ogre: maintaining the appearance of choice over a de facto linear structure (a controversial variant, bordering on masked railroading)

**System-agnostic:** yes — Fully transferable. "Prep situations, not plots" applies as well to a sim/OSR game (e.g. Zombiology: you prepare the state of the epidemic, the NPCs, the resources, and the default degradation, not the story) as to a PbtA. Nuance: PbtA/narrativist games often integrate the open situation into their rules (fronts, threats, clocks, MC principles like "play to find out what happens"), making the technique near-mandatory; sim/traditional games leave it to the GM's choice. The chosen structure (rail/sandbox) remains independent of the system.

**Forge equivalent.** In the Forge corpus: the "open situation" corresponds to preparation by Bangs (Ron Edwards, Sorcerer) — charged conflict-seeds that force a PC to make a revealing choice without dictating its outcome. More broadly, this falls under a narrativist "Story Now" agenda (the story emerges in play via the PCs' choices) as opposed to the railroad, often associated with a "participationism" drift (the players accept the GM's storyline) or "illusionism" (the GM masks the absence of real choice). The sandbox corresponds rather to a simulationist-type "exploration."

## Moving from scenario to campaign (long-haul campaign prep)

**Definition.** A preparation technique that consists of designing not a closed sequence of events (a "plot"), but a living and evolving situation at the scale of the campaign: a network of threats, factions, and NPCs pursuing their own goals, who keep acting independently of the characters. The GM prepares reusable tools (relationship map, fronts/threats with their deadlines, timelines, campaign status document) rather than a pre-written story. The campaign then emerges from the collision between these autonomous forces and the players' choices, and the prep renews itself from session to session instead of being consumed linearly.

**In preparation.** 1) Replace the plot with a campaign SITUATION: pose an unstable status quo and forces that want to change it. 2) Map the actors: draw a relationship map connecting PCs and NPCs by family ties, debts, rivalries, loves — without dictating the action. 3) Structure the threats as "fronts": a campaign front (the background threat, slow but heavy with consequences — e.g. the dark god behind the orcs) overseeing several adventure fronts (immediate, local threats). To each danger, attach "grim portents" (the chain of events that will occur if no one intervenes) leading to an "impending doom" (the final catastrophe). 4) Prepare a timeline: what the antagonists do turn by turn if the PCs do not act — which makes the world reactive. 5) Keep a campaign status document (Campaign Status): the day's news, upcoming events, the activities of major NPCs, dangling narrative threads, hotspots — to be updated between each session. 6) Endow each NPC with clear goals and resources so they react organically. 7) Prepare a "bandolier of bangs": a few explosive situations that force the PCs into engaging choices. 8) Limit the number of fronts (≈3 max) and leave room for the players' contribution; at the end of the session, ask "what do you intend to do?" and prepare the next part from there. 9) Advance the portents either descriptively (you saw the change in play) or prescriptively (as a consequence of a failure).

**Variants.**
- Fronts (Dungeon World / Apocalypse World): campaign front + adventure fronts, dangers, grim portents, impending doom — PbtA formalization
- Relationship map + bangs (Sorcerer / Forge): you prepare ties and moments of tension rather than a plot
- Campaign status document (Alexandrian): an evolving tracking of news, NPCs, dangling threads, hotspots
- Scenario timelines: what the antagonists do in the absence of the PCs
- Node-based design (Alexandrian): the campaign as a network of nodes connected by clues (Three Clue Rule)
- Lazy DM / minimalist prep (Sly Flourish): 8 categories of light elements prepared just before the session
- Sandbox / hexcrawl: the campaign situation carried by the geography and the factions rather than by a storyline

**System-agnostic:** yes — The principle (prepare an evolving situation rather than a plot) is fully transferable to any system, including a simulation game like Zombiology: a relationship map, a timeline of NPC/faction actions, a campaign status document, and autonomous threats work whatever the resolution engine. Only the formalization of "Fronts" (dangers / grim portents / impending doom) comes from the PbtA family (Apocalypse World, Dungeon World); it nonetheless imports very well into a sim game, where it serves as a threat schedule. The "bangs" and the "relationship map" come from the Forge movement (Sorcerer) but are themselves also agnostic.

**Forge equivalent.** Relationship Map + Bangs of Sorcerer (Ron Edwards): you prepare a network of characters and "bangs" (crisis moments forcing moral choices), never a plot. It is the direct ancestor of PbtA Fronts and the "Forge" formulation of the same idea: prepare a situation charged with tension, not a story. To be related also to the Forge distinction between situation prep and railroading/illusionism.

**Sources:** [The Alexandrian — Don't Prep Plots](https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/4147/roleplaying-games/dont-prep-plots) · [The Alexandrian — Don't Prep Plots: Prepping Scenario Timelines (Campaign Status Document)](https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/4154/roleplaying-games/dont-prep-plots-prepping-scenario-timelines) · [Dungeon World SRD — Fronts (Campaign Fronts, Adventure Fronts, Grim Portents, Impending Doom)](https://www.dungeonworldsrd.com/gamemastering/fronts/)

## Adapting a work (film, novel) into an RPG scenario

**Definition.** Transforming a pre-existing fiction (film, novel, series, comic) into playable material for an RPG table. The key principle: you do not adapt the plot (the sequence of events the work's characters live through), but the SITUATION that underlies it — setting, actors present, stakes, latent tensions — to make it replayable with the PCs' agency. Adapting a work is not about having the players re-enact the story, it is about giving them the ingredients that produced that story and letting them produce their own.

**In preparation.** In preparation, proceed in layers: 1) Clarify the "why" — what precise element of the work do you want at the table (atmosphere, moral dilemma, antagonist, twist, setting)? Anything that does not serve that "why" gets cut. 2) Distill the essence into neutral language (no system name, no work name): summarize the starting situation in one sentence, identify the forces present and what each one wants. 3) Strip the plot: extract the SITUATION (who wants what, who opposes whom, what is about to explode) instead of the sequence of scenes; list the NPCs as active agents with a goal, not as scripted steps. 4) Re-cast: replace the work's protagonist with the PCs and find their natural place in the situation; recycle NPCs already known to the campaign for the roles of patron/antagonist. 5) Secure the investigation/the progression: if the work rests on a revelation, sow at least three clues/paths toward each expected conclusion (the three-clue rule) so as not to depend on the "right" choice. 6) Spot the holes: reread the work/the breakdown, note the points where the PCs will inevitably do otherwise than the heroes and prepare open answers rather than rail corrections. 7) Adapt to the system: cut/transpose the mechanical elements (abilities, threats) to the target game frame.

**Variants.**
- Adaptation by setting: keep only the universe/the place (atmosphere, tone, world rules) and pose a new situation in it — useful when the work's plot is too linear.
- Adaptation by character: extract a single antagonist, mentor, or striking NPC and inject them into an existing campaign, without the rest of the story.
- Adaptation by dilemma/theme: retain the thematic core (temptation, betrayal, sacrifice) and rebuild a local situation that poses it to the PCs.
- Episodic adaptation (TV series): apply an "RPG filter" — heavily script the opening of the first episode then progressively loosen the script in favor of agency.
- Adaptation by 'reskin': keep the structure of a module/film and only repaint the names, the aesthetic, and the stakes to fit the table.
- Transposed adaptation: move the situation into another genre/era (the chamber drama of work A transposed into an orbital station) to mask the source and preserve the surprise.

**System-agnostic:** yes — The technique is transferable to any system, including simulationist (Zombiology, GUMSHOE, BRP): you adapt the situation and the actors, then translate the mechanical aspect into the target system. Usage nuance by family: in simulationist/OSR, the emphasis is on the coherence of the world and the multiple clues (the three-clue rule); in PbtA/narrativist, you rather distill the work into "fronts" and threats endowed with agendas, and the adaptation aims to reproduce the tone and the dramatic questions rather than the detailed setting.

**Forge equivalent.** In the Forge corpus, the direct equivalent is the distinction "prepare the situation, not the plot" (situation vs. plot): you prepare a bundle of exploitable tensions, not a story written in advance. The "Bang" (Sorcerer, Ron Edwards) is its operative tool: distill the source work into pivotal moments that force a significant PC choice. The "No Myth" technique (nothing is fixed until it is put into play) is the extreme opposite that illuminates by contrast: adapting a work amounts to pre-loading "Myth" (canonical material) while letting the plot emerge in play.

## Session zero, social contract, and safety tools (lines & veils, X-card, safety tools)

**Definition.** A set of emotional safety tools that frame what may or may not appear at the table. Session zero is a meeting prior to the campaign where the group establishes expectations, tone, and the social contract (explicit/implicit commitments about how to play together). Lines & veils (Ron Edwards, the Sex & Sorcery supplement for Sorcerer) distinguish "lines" (content totally excluded, which does not exist in the fiction) from "veils" (content tolerated but handled off-screen / only alluded to). The X-card (John Stavropoulos, 2013) is a play tool: a card marked with an X that anyone can touch to immediately remove content, without having to justify it. These tools protect the players and clarify the boundaries of the narrative content.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM: 1) Plans a session zero before the first real session, and places the introduction of the tools BEFORE character creation and worldbuilding. 2) Prepares a menu of potentially sensitive themes specific to their scenario (violence, assaults, phobias, family dynamics, etc.) to kick off the discussion rather than waiting for the players to guess. 3) Drafts their social contract: pace, expected tone, handling of absences, house rules, and a list of the retained safety tools (X-card, lines & veils, etc.). 4) Prepares the material: index cards to note the lines & veils (anonymously if needed), an X-card that is visible and accessible. 5) Writes and rehearses their X-card "introduction speech" — Stavropoulos insists: the speech matters more than the card (ask for collective help, recall "no need to explain why," plan to use it themselves early to normalize it). 6) Adapts their scenario outline: provide fades, ellipses, and alternative content for the veiled topics, and rework the plot upstream so that no line is structurally necessary. 7) Provides an update mechanism: the boundaries may evolve during the campaign, to be re-discussed at the start or end of the session.

**Variants.**
- Lines & veils written anonymously on index cards collected by the GM, rather than out loud, to reduce social pressure
- Simple X-card (touch = removal) vs enriched versions: Flag ("this makes me uncomfortable") and Replace ("put this content instead")
- O-card (Kira Magrann): a card with X on the front / O on the back, the O signaling that you want MORE of this content, not only less
- Script Change (Brie Beau Sheldon): a video metaphor — pause, rewind (go back and replay differently), fast-forward (skip ahead in fast motion), instant replay, resume
- Open Door / free exit: leave the table at any time without justification
- Stoplight (green/orange/red) and discreet check-ins (raised finger) to gauge comfort without interrupting the fiction
- Post-session debrief tools (e.g. roses & thorns) to adjust the safety tools over the course of the campaign

**System-agnostic:** yes —

**Forge equivalent.** The Social Contract as theorized at the Forge: the set of explicit and implicit agreements of the group that underpins any game, of which lines & veils from Edwards's corpus directly partake. A link with the Lumpley Principle (system = the way the group agrees on what happens in the fiction): the safety tools are a negotiated formalization of that agreement. The X-card and Script Change are later than the Forge but belong to its "diaspora."

## Drawing on a theme to generate prep (theme-driven prep)

**Definition.** A preparation method where the GM first sets a theme (central idea, moral or emotional tension, atmosphere) then deduces from it the whole of the play material — places, NPCs, conflicts, stakes, encounters. The theme acts as a guiding filter: each prepared element is retained or rejected according to whether it serves the theme or not. It is often expressed in short form (3 to 5 evocative words) or as a central question/statement (e.g. "Is friendship worth dying for?") that gives coherence to everything prepared, instead of piling up heterogeneous elements.

**In preparation.** 1) Formulate the theme in an evocative and compact way ("Pact with the devil," "loyalty has a price," rather than "negotiation"). Draw it if possible from what the players have signaled they want to explore (answers, character sheets, ends of previous sessions). 2) Decline it into a dramatic question or a tension (Premise) that the game will put to the test. 3) Generate the content THROUGH the theme: list places (e.g. d10), NPCs, factions, objects, dangers, and keep only those that embody or pressurize the theme; reskin the generic elements so they refer back to it. 4) Design situations/scenes or "encounters" that force the PCs to take a position on the theme (dilemmas, costly choices), without scripting the outcome. 5) Use the theme as a relevance test for any later addition (treasure, peril, subplot): if it does not serve the theme, you cut it or link it to the characters' background. The deliverable is not a linear scenario but a thematically coherent toolbox, ready to be played by improvisation.

**Variants.**
- Prep by dramatic question (Premise): pose a central moral statement/question and decline it into situations that test it (a legacy of the Forge / Egri)
- Theme distilled into 3-5 evocative words serving as a filter for encounter design (Roleplaying Tips approach)
- Theme drawn from the players: extract the theme from the answer to "What do you want to do?" + "Why?" before preparing
- Theme carried by a Front / a Threat in PbtA: the front's impulse embodies the theme and its grim portents unfold its stages
- Theme as tone/genre (fairytale, mystery, tyranny) applied uniformly to each building block of the adventure for cohesion
- Theme + situation (Alexandrian): prepare a thematically charged situation rather than a plot, then let the PCs respond to it

**System-agnostic:** yes — Transferable to any system. The "theme → prep filter → toolbox" logic works as well in a simulation game (e.g. Zombiology: theme "trust is unraveling," from which come NPCs, scarce resources, quarantine dilemmas) as in narration. An intensified form in the PbtA family, where the theme crystallizes structurally in the Fronts/Threats (impulse = thematic driver, grim portents/clocks = a rising of the theme's pressure); and theorized by the Forge under the term Premise for narrativist play. But the technique itself depends on none of these systems.

**Forge equivalent.** Premise (Ron Edwards, after Lajos Egri, "The Art of Dramatic Writing"): a thematic statement or question (e.g. "Are friends worth dying for?") that narrativist play sets itself the goal of "addressing" through play. Narrativist prep consists of preparing Bangs — urgent crises demanding a decision from the PC, with no imposed outcome — that force the exploration of the theme (the "Get to the bangs!" principle). It is the direct Forge equivalent of theme-driven prep: the theme (Premise) precedes and engenders the material, and prep focuses on thematically charged decision moments rather than on a scripted plot.

## Singularizing and making NPCs endearing (traits, voice, details)

**Definition.** A technique consisting of giving each NPC a minimal number of immediately recognizable distinctive markers — a dominant trait, a voice (timbre, delivery, verbal tic, accent), and a physical or behavioral detail — rather than a long biography. One or two salient markers are enough to make a character memorable and to elicit the players' attachment, replacing written depth with a sensory signature that the table remembers.

**In preparation.** In preparation, the GM first constitutes reusable reserves: a list of first names (e.g. 6 male / 6 female, including a few "exotic" ones and a separate list for non-humans), and half a dozen tics / voices / mannerisms ready to draw from (a catchphrase, a facial tic, a hoarse or high-pitched voice, a slow delivery, an odd cadence). For each named NPC they anticipate, they fix ONE dominant trait + ONE vocal signature + ONE memorable physical detail (scar, jewel, garment), and nothing more. They then keep a register of the NPCs encountered (name, place, accent, posting, motivation, quirk, type of relationship) to guarantee consistency and to be able to recycle them as recurring characters. During the session they cross off the names/traits already used. This is deliberately light work: you prepare activatable building blocks, not full sheets.

**Variants.**
- The "three-line sheet" method: name + dominant trait + what they want (instinct/motivation) — enough to improvise the rest
- Non-vocal signature: for a GM uncomfortable with voices, replace the accent with a recurring gesture, a posture, or a manipulated object
- Unique sensory detail: associate each NPC with a single strong sensation (smell, sound, texture) that serves as a memory anchor
- Random reserve: tables of first names + traits + quirks drawn at random to generate an improvised, instantly credible NPC
- Deliberate contrast: choose traits that clash with the expected role (the gentle executioner, the cultured beggar) to increase memorability
- Recurrence and evolution: note the NPCs the players liked in order to bring them back, adding an extra marker at each appearance

**System-agnostic:** yes — Transferable to any system. The technique does not touch the mechanics: it operates at the level of the fiction and the embodiment, so it works as well in PbtA (where NPCs have only a name and "a detail or two") as in a simulationist game like Zombiology, where a survivor or a resource-NPC gains presence at no mechanical cost. Note: PbtA/Dungeon World formalizes it via the Fronts (NPC = name + detail + instinct, completed in play), but it is not a system exclusivity.

**Forge equivalent.** Close to the notion of Color (the fiction details that do not touch the system but dress up and bring the situation to life) and to the Bang applied to characters: a well-singularized NPC becomes a dramatic hook. It also overlaps with the idea that preparation should create "relationship maps" / relationship nodes rather than a fixed scenario — the endearing NPC is a node of the relationship map.
